Q&A

Q1:

Web1.0: This refers to the earliest stage of the internet in the 1990s. Websites in web1.0 were static and primarily provided information to users. Users couldn't interact directly with the site or create their own content. Examples of web1.0 sites include news sites and portals. Web2.0: This refers to the next generation of the internet that began to develop in the early 2000s. In web2.0, websites can be interactive and allow users to create their own content. Users can participate in discussions, upload photos and videos, publish blogs, and write comments. Examples of web2.0 sites include social networking sites like Facebook and Twitter, wikis, and forums. Web3.0: This refers to the latest generation of the internet that is still in development and leading to the discovery of solutions to problems that exist in web2.0. The idea behind web3.0 is to create an internet with a pattern-based intelligence, with the ability to neutralize commercial usage and personal disappointments that have developed in web2.0. By using technologies such as artificial intelligence and blockchain, web3.0 aims to create a more decentralized, secure, and personalized internet experience for users. Examples of web3.0 applications include decentralized finance (DeFi), blockchain-based social networks, and virtual reality worlds.

Q2:

Web1.0: If you searched for "medication side effect" on a web1.0 site, you would likely find a static webpage with information about medication side effects. The page would be written by an expert or organization and would not allow for user-generated content or interaction. The information might be presented in a list or table format, but the user would not be able to easily filter or sort the information. Web3.0: If you searched for "medication side effect" on a web3.0 site, you might find a personalized health platform that uses artificial intelligence and blockchain technology to provide tailored information about medication side effects. The platform might use natural language processing to understand the user's query and provide relevant information in a conversational format. It might also use smart contracts to ensure the privacy and security of the user's health information. The user might be able to interact with the platform through voice commands or a chatbot, and the platform might provide personalized recommendations for medication alternatives or complementary therapies based on the user's health profile and goals.
